Morning routine

5–10 minutes before the day runs away from you

Epictetus taught that we should set the mind before setting foot into the world. These three practices take under 10 minutes and change how the entire day lands.
Morning · 3 min
Premeditatio malorum — negative visualisation
Before starting the day, briefly imagine what could go wrong — traffic, a difficult meeting, a rejected idea, someone being rude. Don't catastrophise. Just acknowledge it calmly as a possibility. This removes the shock of the unexpected and keeps you anchored when reality arrives.
Today I might face: [one challenge]. If it happens, I will respond with patience and reason, not reactivity.
Morning · 2 min
Set an intention — role + virtue
Ask yourself: what role am I playing today? (Engineer, parent, friend, teammate.) What virtue does that role call for — patience, courage, fairness, focused attention? Write one sentence. The Stoics believed that naming your commitment makes you more likely to keep it.
Today, as a [role], I will practice [one virtue] by [specific behavior].
Morning · 2 min
The Stoic question — split your day by control
Look at your top 2–3 tasks or concerns for the day. For each one, split it: what is up to you, and what is not? Commit your mental energy only to the former. This single habit eliminates a large portion of daily anxiety.
Up to me: my effort, my tone, my preparation.
Not up to me: how others react, the outcome, the timeline.

Evening review

5 minutes — pen and paper preferred

Seneca wrote: "I examine my entire day and go back over what I've done and said." Marcus Aurelius filled twelve books doing this. You only need five minutes.
Evening · 3 min
The daily review — Seneca's three questions
Sit quietly before sleep and run through the day honestly — but with the composure of a student, not the harshness of a judge. The goal is clarity, not guilt. Consistent self-examination is what separates intention from character.
1. What did I do well today?
2. Where did I fall short of my own values?
3. What would I do differently tomorrow?
Evening · 2 min
Gratitude audit — negative visualisation in reverse
Imagine losing one thing you took for granted today — your health, your home, a relationship, your ability to do meaningful work. Hold that feeling briefly, then feel the full weight of having it. This is not pessimism. It is the Stoic practice of building genuine, unforced gratitude.
What would today look like without [one thing I have]? I am grateful it was here.

In the moment

Reach for these when something actually happens

The Stoics called these "disciplines of assent" — the ability to pause between what happens and how you respond. This is where Stoicism earns its keep.
On the spot
The pause — create a gap between stimulus and response
When something provokes you — a sharp message, a criticism, an unexpected delay — stop for one breath before reacting. Epictetus called this "withholding assent." You choose whether to let the impression become an action. The pause is the practice. Most damage in life comes from skipping it.
Is this impression (anger, fear, excitement) accurate? Is it mine to act on right now?
On the spot
The view from above — zoom out
When a problem feels overwhelming, mentally pull back: this moment on a timeline of your week, your year, your decade. Marcus Aurelius practiced this constantly. Most of what feels catastrophic today will be invisible in five years. Does it still deserve the same energy?
Will this matter in a year? In five? What would a wiser, calmer version of me do right now?
On the spot
The Stoic archer — do your best, then release the arrow
You control training, aim, and technique. You do not control where the arrow lands. Apply this to work: focus on the quality of your input — the preparation, the honesty, the craft — and accept the output with equanimity. Excellence lives in the effort, not the outcome.
Have I done everything within my power here? Then I release the outcome without resentment.
On the spot
Other-perspective practice — assume the best motive
When someone frustrates you, before reacting, ask: what is the most charitable interpretation of why they did this? Marcus Aurelius wrote this to himself constantly. Nine times out of ten, people are rushed, stressed, or simply unaware — not malicious. The assumption of malice is almost always wrong.
What might [person] be dealing with that I can't see? How would I want to be treated if our roles were reversed?

Stoic response

Pause. Classify. Act on what you control. Release the rest without noise or drama.

Reactive response

React immediately. Let the emotion decide. Regret it later. Repeat tomorrow.

Control check

Tap each item — is it truly within your power?

The opening line of Epictetus' Enchiridion: "Some things are in our control and others are not." This is the whole philosophy in one sentence. Use this tool whenever you feel anxious or stuck.
My effort and the quality of my work
Up to me — my craft, my commitment
Whether my manager approves or praises me
Not up to me — their judgment, their feelings
How I respond when things don't go my way
Up to me — my reaction is always mine
What other people think or say about me
Not up to me — their opinions are theirs
Whether I seek feedback and act on it honestly
Up to me — curiosity and growth are choices
Economic conditions, company decisions above me
Not up to me — the system, not me
My values — what I stand for, how I treat people
Always up to me — protect this above all
The outcome of a project once I've done my part
Not up to me — release it, archer-style
Whether I exercise, rest, and care for my body today
Up to me — the foundation everything rests on

Weekly practice

Pick one per week and do it fully

The Stoics believed philosophy was not something you studied — it was something you practiced. These weekly exercises build the muscle of equanimity over time.
Weekly
Voluntary discomfort — practice small hardship
Once a week, intentionally choose a mild discomfort: skip coffee, take a cold shower, skip a meal, sit in silence for 20 minutes without your phone. Not for punishment — but to remind yourself that you can handle difficulty. And to appreciate comfort with genuine gratitude instead of entitlement.
This week I will voluntarily go without: [one comfort]. I will notice what changes in my mind.
Weekly
Letter to your wiser self
Write a short letter from the wisest, most grounded version of yourself to your current self, about one challenge you're facing. What would that version say about your fears? Your attachments? Your options? This is what Marcus Aurelius was doing in his Meditations — he never meant them to be published. They were letters to himself.
Dear present-me — about [challenge], here is what I think you're missing...
Weekly
The role audit — are you actually showing up?
List your key roles: colleague, parent, partner, friend, citizen. For each one, rate yourself honestly: did you behave according to your best values this week? Where did you drift? No self-flagellation — just honest accounting. A student who never checks their work never improves.
As a [role], this week I showed up well by: [action]. I fell short by: [action]. Next week I will: [intention].
Weekly
Philosophical reading — 10 minutes, slowly
Read a short passage from a primary Stoic text. Read slowly. Read twice. Ask: what is the author actually saying beneath the words? Where does it touch my life right now? The goal isn't to finish the book. It's to absorb one idea so thoroughly that it changes how you act.
Start here: Meditations by Marcus Aurelius, Letters from a Stoic by Seneca, or the Enchiridion by Epictetus.

Stoic journal

Write here — your entries stay private on this page

Marcus Aurelius wrote his Meditations entirely as private journal entries — never meant for anyone else. This is the tradition. Write honestly, without performance.

What could go wrong today? (and how will I handle it?)
